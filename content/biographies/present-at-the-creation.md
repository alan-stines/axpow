Sally Bateman Morgan joined the ranks of "The Greatest Generation" at an early age, long before what should have been her time. World War II turned the lives and fortunes of many people in wholly unexpected ways, and Sally's story is among the most extraordinary.

The daughter of a Chinese mother and an American father, she was born in 1930 in Tientsin, China. In late 1941, as war spread across the Pacific, she and her brothers were brought toward safety, only to become civilian prisoners when Manila fell under Japanese control.

Sally and her family were imprisoned first at Santo Tomas and later at Los Banos. They survived on meager rations and endured years of uncertainty. She was finally liberated on February 23, 1945, by the 11th Airborne.

In later years, Sally became one of AXPOW's most dedicated leaders. She joined in 1973 and served in multiple national roles, including National Adjutant/Treasurer and National Quartermaster. She helped modernize records, strengthened operations, and supported the organization through decades of transition.

Her story reflects the enduring purpose of AXPOW: preserving memory, honoring service, and ensuring future generations understand the cost of freedom.
